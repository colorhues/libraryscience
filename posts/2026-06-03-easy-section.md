---
title: Reorganizing the Easy section
date: 2026-06-03
tags: [cataloging]
---

Easy books are shelved by the author's last name, not by Dewey. That's a small thing that turns out to matter a lot.

The "Easy" designation in a primary-school library means picture books and early readers — kids who are still building up to chapter books. Dewey decimal classification doesn't really map to this material, which is by definition pre-classification. So you shelve by author last name and hope kids remember author names. They mostly don't, but they remember covers.

Reshelving 200+ picture books taught me more about K-2 reading habits than any book about library science could have:

- The popular books are physically beat up in a specific way. Spine creased, corners rounded, sometimes a small tear near the front. You can tell which books are loved by what they look like, more reliably than by checkout records.
- Some authors have a *lot* more books than I'd expected. Mo Willems alone took up almost half a shelf.
- The "I want this one because of the cover" effect is real and powerful. The librarian rotates display copies of certain books face-out, and those books get checked out at a much higher rate, regardless of content.
- A book misshelved by one letter is functionally lost. A book misshelved by two letters might as well not exist.

The reshelving project surfaced about a dozen books that didn't belong in Easy at all — chapter books that had been shelved there by mistake. Those go to Fiction, with proper Dewey/Cutter labels. That's a separate problem for a separate day.
